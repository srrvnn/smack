Distribution of euro bills begins

By Batuk Gathani

BRUSSELS, AUG. 31. Major euro-zone economies, led by France and Italy, have commenced the first stage of massive logistical operations needed to distribute new ``euro'' currency notes and coins. Over the weekend, France shipped out more than 100 tonnes of coins to warehouses in major cities in the country. In Italy, two trains brought in tonnes of coins to Milan - the country's commercial capital - guarded by armed police.

In anticipation of the launch of the euro on January 1, 2002, distribution of coins and notes in the euro-zone economies has begun in earnest. On Thursday, the European Central Bank will display the final design and security features of the euro notes. In the 12-nation euro-zone region, most banks, financial institutions and prominent trading companies and retail outlets now issue receipts, statements and invoices in local currencies but prominently display `euro' equivalent numbers. The ECB this week will also launch a major pan-European information campaign for the new currency.

The euro's 10 per cent appreciation against the dollar over the last eight weeks has further boosted euro's strength. In financial markets there is speculation that on Thursday, with the launch of the final design of the euro notes, the ECB may also announce another quarter per cent cut in the interest rate. The proposed cut from the current 4.5 per cent would be the first since May 10, compared to the U.S. Federal Reserve's seven cuts in recent months, amounting to a cumulative three percentage point. The ECB's decision to cut the interest rate may depend on the latest fiscal data released by the Federal Reserve on Tuesday.

With growing uncertainty about the health of the U.S. economy and speculation in markets that the dollar is still overvalued, European analysts are predicting that traditional trans-Atlantic investment flows may turn towards euro-zone countries. The international funds normally lead the way to highlight new financial trends. The euro-zone becomes more attractive as few European stock funds have performed well in recent weeks. At the same time, the euro-zone region is also passing through a phase of economic downturn with many hiccups, especially on the employment front.

Several European and multi-national companies have announced plans to lay off more workers due to shrinking profits and dwindling consumer demand. Although there is cautious optimism about the euro, one sparrow does not make spring and most analysts believe, it is too early to suggest that the euro's rise against the dollar could continue indefinitely. It is certainly not a sure thing and a major imponderable on the investment front is that if the U.S. economy picks up, investment cash may again flow back to the U.S.

The euro-zone region is not rated as a bed of roses for investment. For example, a prominent European fund fell by less than 22 per cent last year and is down nearly 15 per cent this year. In June, relatively lower unemployment and higher consumer confidence in the euro-zone region first triggered euro's advance against the dollar. But since then, the unemployment level has increased to above 9.5 per cent and consumer confidence is again sagging.
