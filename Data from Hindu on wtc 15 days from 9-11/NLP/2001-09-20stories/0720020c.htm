Anand brings joy to special children

By Our Special Correspondent

MUMBAI, SEPT. 19 Grandmaster Viswanathan Anand encountered fans of a different kind in Mumbai. The Indian chess maestro spent delightful, fascinating moments with the deaf and deaf-blind students of Helen Keller Institute in Byculla on Wednesday, the interaction moderated by volunteer Khushnaz in sign language. The students expressed their admiration for his sharp brain and achievements, the Grandmaster was only too eager to answer their queries in a meeting arranged by NIIT.

GM Anand, who spent time with the Institute's students who demonstrated their grasp of braille chess on computers donated to their institution, then spoke about how chess can bring about a change in children's lives, especially those less fortunate ones. ``Chess is not a sport where the physical aspect plays a large part, so it is possible for children like the ones at the institute here to play. Computer chess will in fact bring out the best in them, make them mentally sharper,'' he said.

The conversation then turned to his experiences on the World Trade Centre, where the world championship match against Garry Kasparov was played. ``The match was played exactly six years ago,'' said GM Anand, referring to the September 11, 1995 clash on the 107th floor of the WTC which Kasparov won. Asked whether he felt a ``top of the world'' feeling playing up there, he replied: ``I got that feeling going up to the 107th floor, not when the match was on. It was played in a room like anywhere else, where you don't get an idea of the location.''

Expressing his shock at last Tuesday's terrorist strike which brought down the twin towers of the WTC, Anand dwelt on the city, saying that he became aware that New York was a ``dynamic city''. Asked for his views on the raging controversy over the issue of dilution of Arjuna awards, the best known name in Indian chess was of the view that he felt glad when the award was conferred on him. ``I am in touch with Indian sportspersons and their achievements on the Net, because our schedules are different.''

Anand will be felicitating NIIT's Bhavishya Jyoti scholars on Thursday. The scheme for poor, meritorious students and applicable for NIIT's Futurz programmes like D-NIIT and G- NIIT, has crossed the 100,000 mark in terms of the number of beneficiaries. The Indian Grandmaster, who endorses NIIT through advertisements, now a prominent feature on television and in newspapers, feels chess as a sport and Information Technology have a lot in common.

Surprisingly, he has not tied up for endorsements with any other IT major in India or Europe where he is a very visible name. ``They all know I am with NIIT, maybe that is the reason,'' said Anand, surely one of India's most famous sporting ambassadors, cutting across generation and language barriers with his mastery over the black and white squares.
