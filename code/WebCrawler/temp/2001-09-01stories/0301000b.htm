Taliban post for Osama condemned

By Vladimir Radyuhin

MOSCOW, AUG. 31. Moscow described the appointment of the Islamic terrorist leader Osama Bin Laden as head of the Taliban military forces as fresh evidence of the movement's expansionist ambitions. Russia also drew attention to the fact that the Taliban appointed the Uzbek militant, Djuma Namangani, as Bin Laden's deputy.

``Such steps give vivid proof of international concerns that a centre of international terrorism and a bridgehead for expansion of militant extremism and separatism far beyond the region are being set up on the Taliban-controlled territory of Afghanistan under the smokescreen of pseudo-religious values,'' the Russian Foreign Ministry said in a statement.

Djuma Namangani led two incursions into the former Soviet republics of Kyrgyzstan and Uzbekistan in 2000 and 1999 with the declared purpose of establishing an Islamic Khalifat in Central Asia. The attacks prompted Russia and some Central Asian States to set up a rapid deployment force in the region this year. Moscow urged the international community to take ``decisive action'' to oppose the Taliban threat and to implement the U.N. Security Council resolutions calling for an arms embargo against the Taliban and for dismantling the movement's terrorist infrastructure.

``The world community should firmly tell the Taliban movement that it will not allow the long-suffering land of Afghanistan to be turned into a spewing ground for transborder terrorism and instability,'' the Russian Foreign Ministry statement said.
