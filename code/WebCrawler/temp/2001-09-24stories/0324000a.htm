Pak. Govt. will remain stable, says Powell

WASHINGTON, SEPT. 23. The U.S. Secretary of State, Gen. Colin Powell, today lauded Pakistan's decision to support the U.S. offensive against Osama bin Laden as ``courageous'' and predicted that the Pakistan Government would remain stable. Gen. Powell also said he had no concerns about risks to Pakistan's nuclear defence programme.

``Everything I have seen over the past two weeks convinces me that Gen. Musharraf made a courageous decision and he did it with full awareness of the potential domestic consequences,'' he told NBC's ``meet the press'' programme. ``He is supported by all his military commanders and others in the Government,'' he said adding the target of any U.S. military action in Afghanistan would be against Osama, not the people of Afghanistan.

``We are not interested in going after the Afghan people. We are interested in going after Osama and his network, murderers of over 6,000 people in this incident,'' he said.

Gen. Powell cautioned against creating any link between Osama and Muslims around the world, saying, ``Frankly we should stop finding some way to generate sympathy for these murderers.

Meanwhile, a team of U.S. military and intelligence officials is headed for Pakistan to inspect the facilities the country had made for possible strikes on terrorist hideouts in Afghanistan. Officials here said the lifting of economic sanctions against Pakistan cleared the way for the visit of the team. The officials said the U.S. will keep Pakistan abreast of the latest developments in the investigation into the September 11 terrorist attacks. This was in response to Pakistan's request that the U.S. make public its evidence against Osama and that any action taken be under the framework of the U.N.

Meanwhile, the Indian Ambassador, Mr. Lalit Mansingh, reiterated India's commitment to aid the U.S. efforts to fight terrorism.

- Reuters

Pak. awaiting U.S. response

PTI reports from Delhi

The Pakistan Foreign Minister, Mr. Abdul Sattar, has said Pakistan is waiting for clear indication from the U.S. about how precisely Pakistan would be able to help Washington in its fight against terrorism.

``We have assured the world community of our cooperation in the fight against terrorism and we have informed the U.S. that we will cooperate,'' Mr. Sattar told the BBC.

He said ``so far the discussions have been in general terms. These have involved the questions of sharing information and logistic assistance.''

``We are awaiting a delegation from the U.S. with which we will enter into discussion with regard to concrete and precise aspects in which Pakistan might be in a position to cooperate,'' he said.

Speaking to Reuters, Mr. Sattar welcomed the U.S. decision to lift economic sanctions imposed in 1998 after Pakistan carried out nuclear tests. The move was widely anticipated after Gen. Musharraf pledged to help Washington in its fight against international terrorism.

The White House announced that the U.S. President, Mr. George W. Bush, had decided to waive sanctions because they were not ``in the security interests of the U.S.'' Mr. Sattar noted, however, that the order did not include sanctions relating to the 1999 coup that brought Gen. Musharraf to power. A Government source said this ``half measure'' would disappoint many people in Pakistan. ``This is a first stop, but it is not enough,'' the former Information Minister, Mr. Mushahid Hussain, said.
